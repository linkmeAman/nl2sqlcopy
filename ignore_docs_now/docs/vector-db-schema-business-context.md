# Vector DB Schema And Business Context

This file is an embedding-oriented summary of the backend data model and business logic. It combines the source-derived relationship map from `database-table-relations.md` with the broader schema signals visible in `mysql_schema_export.txt`. The application is centered around three schemas. `pf_TickleRight_9210` stores tenant business data such as contacts, inquiries, members, batches, attendance, invoices, payments, reports, and branch-level settings. `pf_central` stores shared platform data such as clients, users, permissions, request queues, biometric devices, modules, and assessment metadata. `pf_admin` is used mainly for admin-side coupon and advertisement availability logs. The codebase behaves like a legacy PHP monolith that reads and writes tables directly and also depends heavily on SQL views as a practical read model.

The most important modeling rule for this system is that many relationships are real in application behavior even when they are not enforced by foreign keys in MySQL. The source-derived documentation shows that the main tenant business model is tied together by joins, subqueries, and view definitions rather than by explicit database constraints. The schema export confirms that some newer or more isolated parts of the database do use foreign keys, such as `b2b_invoice_items.invoice_id -> b2b_invoices.id` and several central user tables like `user_bid.user_id -> user.id`, `user_device.user_id -> user.id`, `user_permission.user_id -> user.id`, and `user_social.user_id -> user.id`. For the core legacy business model, however, relations should usually be interpreted as application-level or view-defined unless a constraint is explicitly present in schema SQL.

The CRM and enrollment lifecycle starts with `contact`, `inquiry`, and `followup`. `contact` is the core person record and is reused across leads, members, employees, invoices, and attendance. `inquiry` represents a lead or admission interest tied to a contact. It stores lead source information such as `heard_from`, source, employee ownership, and related marketing details. `heard_from` is used as a lookup-like source channel for inquiries, and scheduler/report code frequently groups inquiries by `heard_from`. `followup` is the action and reminder layer on top of inquiries and member/payment events. A followup belongs to a contact, can point to a parent followup, and can also point to different business objects through the `master` and `master_id` pattern. In practice that means followup records are polymorphic: some followups point to inquiry context, some to payment context, and some to invoice item context. Business logic uses this for counselling, lead nurturing, irregular attendance reminders, expiry flows, birthdays, and other engagement actions.

The membership model is built around `member`, which is effectively the enrolled or registered person state for a `contact`. The code and views consistently connect `member.contact_id` back to `contact.id`, and invoicing logic also treats `member` as the active status layer on top of invoice history. A contact can have inquiry history before becoming a member, and a member’s active or inactive behavior is influenced by invoice, invoice item, renewal, dropout, and attendance logic. This means the business process is not simply “contact becomes member once”; instead, the same contact can move through lead, conversion, renewal, pause, dropout, and reactivation states over time.

The teaching and scheduling model is built around `batch`, `batch_time`, `batch_employee`, `session`, `venue`, `branch`, and `level`. A `batch` represents a class or program cohort. It is categorized by `category_id`, attached to a `branch` through `bid`, and in many flows attached to a `venue` through `venue_id`. `batch_time` stores the recurring day and time schedule for the batch. `batch_employee` acts like the assignment bridge between a batch and the employee contact teaching or owning that batch. `session` is the dated execution of a batch class and links back to the batch and to session master definitions. Many scheduling and attendance decisions are made against the derived views rather than directly against raw tables. In operational terms, a batch is the planned class structure, batch time defines recurrence, batch employee defines who teaches it, and session defines what actually happens on a given date.

Attendance business logic depends on the class structure described above. `attendance` records connect a contact to a batch and sometimes to a specific session. The codebase repeatedly joins attendance to contact and batch, and `attendance_cont_view` enriches attendance with contact, batch, and session metadata for reporting and workflows. Attendance behavior is also controlled by per-branch settings in `attendance_settings`, which appears to be a branch-scoped configuration table keyed in practice by `bid`. Those settings drive logic like biometric configuration, session deduction and reversion, negative attendance, backdated punching, and irregular followup automation. In business terms, attendance is not just a log of presence; it is tied to package consumption, teaching schedules, automation, and downstream communication.

Revenue logic is built around `invoice`, `invoice_item`, `payment`, and related service definitions. An `invoice` belongs to a contact. Each invoice has one or more `invoice_item` rows, and each invoice item ties the sale to a `category`, `service`, and often a `batch`. This is where most package entitlements are modeled: start date, end date, number of sessions, consumed sessions, renewals, changes, and pricing details are stored or derived from invoice item rows. `payment` belongs to the invoice and holds the money movement side such as amount, receipt, balance impact, pay mode, and payment metadata. In business terms, the invoice is the commercial document, invoice items are the sold packages or services, and payment records show how those invoices were settled.

`service` is one of the key bridge concepts between CRM, teaching, and billing. Invoice items point to services, and service definitions point further into curriculum structure through module, topic, and session master references. The source-derived views show that a service can point to a tenant `module`, a tenant `topic`, and a tenant `session_master`. This means a sold package is not just financial; it also carries curriculum structure. That relationship is especially visible in `invoice_invoiceitem_view` and `invoice_invoiceitem_module_view`, which expand commercial rows into curriculum-aware reporting rows. `service_makeup` also appears in invoice and reporting flows as an auxiliary table for makeup-session related behavior.

The most important read models in the system are views. `invoice_invoiceitem_view` is the core commercial-operational view that combines invoice, invoice item, category, service, contact, member, batch, and curriculum metadata into a single row shape used all over the backend. `batch_view` combines batch, batch time, employee assignment, category, and contact display information. `session_batch_view` combines session, batch, employee contact data, branch, and session master metadata. `batch_employee_time_view` is used heavily by attendance and scheduling code because it merges recurring batch structure with assigned employees and possible session overrides. `invoice_invoiceitem_module_view` extends invoice coverage into module-level curriculum coverage by connecting invoice items to service module structures and session ranges. From a vector-retrieval standpoint, these views are often more important than the raw tables because they represent how the application actually reads business context.

Branch and venue logic appears throughout both teaching and payment flows. `branch` is the practical operational scope for many settings, reports, attendance rules, invoice numbering, and payment routing. `venue` is used mostly for offline operations, venue rent calculations, room/classroom mapping, and venue-based revenue sharing. `batch.venue_id` is not strongly documented as a database-enforced foreign key, but it is repeatedly used in batch, venue, and reporting queries. `payment_settings`, `invoice_settings`, and `attendance_settings` all behave like branch-scoped configuration tables. This makes branch the main business partition inside a tenant database, with venue acting as a physical-delivery or offline-execution dimension under that branch context.

The reporting subsystem is metadata-driven. `report` defines a report, `report_column` defines its visible fields, `report_filter` defines default filters, and `report_order` defines ordering. Controllers build structured reports by joining these metadata tables and then applying them to a target table or view. This means reporting logic is not hardcoded for every screen; instead, many list/report screens are configured through database metadata and then executed dynamically. For embeddings, this is important because report tables are not only analytics structures; they are part of the runtime application behavior that shapes what data users see.

The shared platform schema `pf_central` connects tenant data to cross-tenant identities and services. `client_db` represents a client or tenant database entry and is used throughout the backend whenever code maps a tenant db name to a client id. `user` is the shared login or application user model. It links to `client_db` through `client_id` and often links back to tenant `contact` through `contact_id`. That allows the system to connect a global or platform user account to a person record inside the tenant database. `user_bid`, `user_social`, and `user_permission` expand this model with branch access, email/social identity, and module permissions. `client_module` links a client to an enabled module, and `user_permission.cm_id` links a user permission row to the client-module row that granted it. In practical business terms, central tables define which users exist, which client they belong to, which branches they can act in, and which modules they can use.

`request` in `pf_central` is a key automation and queueing concept. The request table does not point to a single entity type. Instead, it stores `action_id`, `client_id`, a `table_name`, and a `row_id`. Scripts that generate SMS and email requests create queue rows that say, in effect, “perform this action for this tenant and this record in this table or view.” This makes the request model polymorphic and workflow-oriented rather than strictly relational. It is used for notifications, scheduled communications, session-related actions, invoice email flows, and followup automation. `action` acts as the action catalog behind these queued requests.

Biometric and hardware integration also runs through `pf_central`. `bio_device` links back to `client_db` and is repeatedly used to resolve which tenant database and branch should be used when attendance is coming from a biometric or device flow. Additional device-like central tables such as `raspberry_pi` appear in batch and venue workflows. The important takeaway is that some hardware and system integrations are not modeled inside the tenant schema itself; instead, they are resolved centrally and then mapped into tenant operations.

Assessment logic spans both tenant and central schemas. Tenant-side records such as `assessment_log`, `assessment_log_value`, `assessment_schedule`, and workout-related tables hold contact-specific or execution-specific data. Central tables such as `assessment_group`, `assessment_task`, and `assessment_task_value` define the reusable assessment taxonomy and task catalog. `assessment_template_task` behaves like a junction between templates and tasks. In business terms, this means the tenant stores actual scheduled assessments and captured responses, while the central schema stores the reusable definition of what an assessment is composed of.

The schema export shows that the database also contains many specialized or newer subsystems beyond the core legacy model. Examples include `b2b_invoices` and related `b2b_invoice_items`, bank and passbook tables, RBAC tables, marketing/social integration tables, and more modern user/chat tables. These newer areas are more likely to use explicit foreign keys. For vector retrieval, they should be treated as supporting domains unless the consuming use case specifically targets those modules. The primary business backbone for day-to-day operations still runs through contact, inquiry, followup, member, batch, attendance, invoice, payment, branch, and central client/user/request tables.

The admin schema `pf_admin` is light in direct operational coupling from the scanned application code. The clearest confirmed tables are `coupon_avail_log` and `advertisement_avail_log`. Both are written from tenant-side flows and connect an admin availability log back to a tenant contact and a central client id. This means `pf_admin` acts more like a cross-tenant promotional and campaign logging area than a core operational system of record.

For embedding and retrieval, the cleanest mental model is this: `contact` is the person, `inquiry` is the lead, `followup` is the engagement workflow, `member` is the enrolled state, `batch` is the class/program container, `session` is the dated class event, `attendance` is the participation record, `invoice` and `invoice_item` are the commercial entitlement model, `payment` is the settlement model, `branch` is the main operational partition, `venue` is the offline execution location, `client_db` is the tenant registry, `user` and related central tables define identity and permissions, and `request` is the automation queue that ties tenant business records to communication and action workflows.

The main caution for downstream consumers is that the effective business model of this system lives partly in base tables and partly in views. Any retrieval or reasoning layer should prefer business interpretations that follow the documented views and repeated controller join patterns, not only raw table names. It should also avoid assuming that a repeated join implies a real database-enforced foreign key, because the legacy tenant model is mostly source-defined rather than constraint-defined.
